### H2
What the session is

**Body, paragraph 1 (what it is):**
A guided exposure to vibration. The practitioner works with calibrated singing bowls, gong, tuning forks, and toned voice, sometimes layered, sometimes singular. Guests lie supine on a mat or recline in a chair; nothing is asked of the body other than to receive.

**Body, paragraph 2 (why):**
Across a stay, the work is small and constant. Some guests come for sleep that holds; some for the quiet long tones leave behind; some for a nervous system that loosens its grip after weeks at speed.

**Body, paragraph 3 (how it feels):**
Long tones that hold, short tones that pass through. The body settles before the mind does. Silence holds for a long beat after the last tone, then the hour closes.

## Module 9: FAQ
*Flag: NEW: module — SEO ADD*

### H2
Common questions

**FAQ Q+A 1:**
Q: Is Sound Healing suitable for children? A: Yes. The Brochure category is "Energy and sound healing for all ages", and a family-adapted version of the session sits in the As a Family inclusions catalogue. Children attend with a guardian.

**FAQ Q+A 2:**
Q: Do I need to do anything during the session? A: Lie still and listen. The work is non-contact and non-verbal. Guests are not asked to focus, breathe in a particular way, or follow an instruction; the room does the work.

**FAQ Q+A 3:**
Q: Is Sound Healing the same as Sound Bath Meditation? A: They sit alongside each other in the Mindfulness category. Sound Bath Meditation is led with a guided meditation thread; Sound Healing carries the work through the instruments alone. Both run fifty minutes.

**FAQ Q+A 4:**
Q: Can Sound Healing help with sleep? A: Many guests notice their sleep change after a session. Like the rest of the work at Jayasom, the gain may build across a stay rather than from a single visit.

**FAQ Q+A 5:**
Q: Where does Sound Healing draw from? A: Energy and Sound is one of the Therapeutic Pillars at Jayasom. The instruments draw on Tibetan and Himalayan vibrational traditions; the integration with the wider practice is led by the resident clinical team.
